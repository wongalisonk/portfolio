UN1FIED Smartwatch

Exploring new possibilities from your wrist

See the detail

Stats

Duration

Autumn 2016 (Oct. to Dec.)

Roles

User Researcher, UX Designer, UI Designer, Product Designer

Team

Kevin Chiu, Vansh Gambhir, Sean Ker, Daniel Hoang, Jeff Ma, Anirudh Ramanathan & Shang Yang

Problem

People are adverse against smartwatches due to preconceptions that they are an inferior version of their smartphone. The small screen and decreased interaction capabilities are off putting. Our team was tasked with creating the stock applications for an entirely new smartwatch of our own creation.

Solution

UN1FIED Smartwatch is has 13 applications designed to integrate with smartphone seamlessly by enhancing the most commonly used functions of a phone. By making the most frequent interactions as simple as possible, people can leave their phones in their pockets and go about their day.

Objectives

  1. Design the operating system (i.e. stock applications) for a smartwatch that is intuitive, versatile, easy to use, and beautiful.
  2. Create an experience that enhances a userâ€™s experience with their smartphone by optimizing the most common functionalities.

Step 1 Research

The goal of this phase was to better understand the perception of smartwatches of both smartwatch owners and nonowners. The survey had a total of 74 respondents, 11 of which were smartwatch owners.

Goals for Smartwatch Owners

The most important insights that we were looking for from the survey data were:
(1) the most commonly used features/apps

(2) the features users liked the most and why and, (3) the features that needed to be changed or added From the survey, we also hoped to discover the most popular smartwatches to conduct an informal competitor analysis.

Goals for Non-Smartwatch Owners

From the survey population that did not own smartwatches the most important question we wanted to answer was why not? From the survey, we wanted to learn why people did not own a smartwatch (besides costs) and what features or apps would appeal to them in a smartwatch.

Insights

Most people perceived that smartwatches were meant to perform exactly as a smartphone, but with a less seamless (i.e. clunky) experience. As a result, people's understanding of a smartwatch was "why would I want to waste money on an inferior device when my phone does it all better."

The biggest turn off for people from both target populations was, unsurprisingly, the small screen size. People also expressed a desire to spend as little time interacting with a smartwatch as possible.

The top desired apps smartwatch nonowners wanted were navigation apps (such as Google maps, bus times, and travel times) and health-based apps (calorie tracking, pedometer, and heart rate monitor).

Summary

Two overarching ideas carried us over to the next phase of our project:

  1. Users don't want a smartwatch to replace their cellphone, but rather to enhance their experience with one and optimize the functionality.

  2. A smartwatch does not need to have all the functionality of a smartphone, but rather optimizes the most common tasks that a smartphone does by making it faster.

Step 2 Design Language

Guidelines

To achieve consistency in our designs across our team, we sat down and created a design language and a style guide that would facilitate our design process.

Our design language consisted of four concepts created from our user research and brainstorming session. It was used to evaluate the effectiveness of our design in achieving user goals.

Adaptable

The ability for users to use their watch in various settings by enabling them to interact with it using either their voice or fingers.

Easy to Use

The learning curve is gradual and the interface is intuitive, enabling users to navigate the watch with only one or two interactions.

Fast

Any goals for the user can be completed in as little number of taps or interactions as possible.

Beautiful

An inviting interface that uses modern UI patterns so the transition from a phone or computer to our watch is seamless and smooth.

Step 3 Style Guide

While the design language was the framework to shape our thinking and approach to the design, the style guide was created to achieve consistency on the smaller details. This included details such as the color palette, typography, iconography, and some general UI patterns.

Step 4 Hardware

A balance of form and functionality was achieved by keeping the hardware as clean and minimal as possible while still allowing users to navigate easily.

To maximize the use of the limiting screen size, the hardware was designed so that all interactions can be completed via the hardware of the watch and not the screen itself.

Step 5 Conceptualization

For each stock application, our team would sit down and sketch out as many ideas as possible for a given app in a 5-minute timeframe. Afterwards, we would meet back up and discuss our ideas as a team. During these meetings we would consider things like, what are the similarities between the designs? are there any unique designs worth considering for the next iteration?, and what features would be unrealistic to implement?

Low-Fidelity Mockups

With 12 apps to tackle in 10 weeks, our team decided to divide and conquer. We split our team of 8 into two smaller teams of 3 and a group of 2. After the initial big team brainstorming session, it was up to the smaller teams to use those ideas and distill them into an initial mockup/prototype.

Big Picture First, Details Later

The first stage was focused on what information would be included and emphasis on user interactions. This stage was focused on evaluating how effective the information flow was in terms of what the user needed. The biggest challenge was striking a balance between providing the user with enough information, but not forcing them to interact with the watch excessively.

Step 6 Iterate

The biggest question I found myself asking myself was "what information can I get rid of? What doesn't the user need?" It was easy to want to cram in as much information as possible across multiple screens. The most important thing was understanding the only information that was necessary was the information a user needed in the moment because if a user wanted more information, they would use their phone instead.

Step 8 Reflection

Lessons Learned

Big teams are hard to work with. Everyone has an opinion and never do we all agree on something unanimously. It takes a group effort to get work done and most of the effort requires constant, open, and honest communication. Also, circular interfaces are really hard to design for.

In the future when working in larger groups, having structured meetings are a necessity. This means going in with goals for the meeting and cutting discussions short if needed.
